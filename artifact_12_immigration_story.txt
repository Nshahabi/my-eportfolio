The Carpet Makers
Neshat Shahabi
Creative Writing Assignment

The workshop smelled like wool and dye, a scent that clung to everything my grandfather owned. Even now years after he passed away I swear I can still smell it on the old sweater my grandmother saved for me. He spent forty years in that small workshop in Tabriz, his fingers moving across the loom with a speed and precision that seemed almost magical to my childs eyes.

Every knot tells a story he used to say in Farsi, his hands never stopping their rhythmic work. I was maybe seven or eight sitting cross legged on a cushion watching him work on a carpet that had already taken him six months to create. This one he pointed to a tiny red knot among hundreds of blues and golds this is the day your mother was born.

I didn't understand then. How could a knot in a carpet represent a day or a moment? But he was serious his weathered face soft with memory.

Your grandmother wanted roses that day he continued. She was in labor for eighteen hours and all she could talk about was how she wanted to plant more roses in the garden. So I used this red see how it catches the light to remember her strength.

My grandfathers carpets hung in homes across Iran and later around the world. Each one took months or even years to complete. Each one was a masterpiece of geometry and color, traditional patterns passed down through generations of carpet makers. But to him they were more than just beautiful objects. They were memory books, story keepers, pieces of our family history woven into thread.

When I was twelve everything changed. My parents made the decision to leave Iran and move to America. I didn't want to go. I had friends, a school I liked and a language I understood perfectly. But more than anything I didn't want to leave my grandparents. My grandfather was getting older and I knew even at twelve that leaving might mean never seeing him again.

The night before we left my grandfather called me into his workshop one last time. The loom sat silent which was unusual. He always had a carpet in progress.

Come here azizam he said using the Farsi word for my dear. He walked over to the corner where several finished carpets were rolled and stacked. He pulled one out smaller than his usual work and unrolled it on the floor.

It was beautiful. Deep blues like the sky at dusk, gold like sunlight on wheat, red like the roses my grandmother grew and green like the trees in our courtyard. The pattern was traditional a medallion in the center surrounded by intricate borders but there was something different about it. As I looked closer I saw tiny imperfections woven throughout. A knot that was slightly the wrong color. A pattern that broke its symmetry for just one small section.

You made mistakes I asked, surprised. My grandfather never made mistakes.

He laughed a sound I can still hear perfectly in my mind. No jaan, not mistakes. Memories. This blue here he pointed to a section near the corner this is from the day you were born. I was so excited I could barely hold the thread steady. And this he moved his finger to a green section this is from the summer you were six and you learned to swim. You remember? You were so proud of yourself.

He showed me a dozen small irregularities each one marking a moment in my life. The day I lost my first tooth. The time I won the school poetry recitation contest. The afternoon I fell off my bike and came home crying. Every significant moment of my childhood was hidden in that carpet disguised as tiny imperfections in an otherwise perfect pattern.

In Persian carpet making he told me we believe that only God is perfect. So we leave intentional imperfections in our work as a sign of humility. But I always liked to make my imperfections mean something. This carpet is for you to take to America. When you feel lost or alone you can look at it and remember where you come from.

I cried. I didn't want to cry because I was trying to be strong but I couldn't help it. He rolled up the carpet carefully and tied it with string.

You know what else he said, his eyes bright despite his age. A carpet is made one knot at a time. You cannot rush it. You cannot skip steps. Each knot has to be tied carefully in its proper place or the whole pattern falls apart.

He put his hand on my shoulder. Your life in America will be like starting a new carpet. Right now you can only see the first few rows of knots and they might not make sense. They might look random or wrong. But trust the process. Keep tying the knots one at a time and eventually a pattern will emerge. It might not be the pattern you expected but it will be yours and it will be beautiful.

We left the next day. The carpet came with us rolled carefully in protective cloth, one of the few precious things we could bring on the plane.

The first year in America was hard. Everything was strange and difficult. I missed my friends, my school, my language. I missed my grandparents most of all. Sometimes I would unroll the carpet in my new bedroom and sit on it running my fingers over the knots trying to feel connected to home.

My grandfather was right though. Life in America was like starting a new carpet. At first nothing made sense. I was learning English, making new friends, trying to understand a culture that was completely foreign to me. Each day felt like tying a knot in the dark not knowing if it was the right color or if it would fit into any larger pattern.

But slowly over months and then years a pattern started to emerge. I got better at English. I made friends who accepted me. I learned to navigate between my Iranian identity and my American one. I found things I was good at and things I enjoyed. The knots started to come together into something I could recognize.

My grandfather passed away when I was fifteen. We couldn't go back for the funeral because of visa complications. I was devastated. I sat in my room for hours just looking at the carpet, crying into its wool.

My grandmother called that night. Through her tears she told me that in his final days my grandfather had talked about carpets. He said to tell you she whispered in Farsi her voice breaking that every carpet has difficult sections. Parts where the pattern gets complex, where your fingers hurt, where you want to give up. But those are often the most beautiful parts when you step back and see the whole thing.

I'm in college now still figuring out who I am and what pattern my life is creating. The carpet hangs on my dorm room wall. My roommate thinks its just a beautiful decoration which it is. But to me its so much more.

Sometimes I'll notice things in the carpet I never saw before a color combination that's particularly striking or a tiny detail in the border that I'd overlooked. It reminds me that life is like that too. You can experience the same moment the same memory and find new meaning in it years later.

I think about my grandfathers hands, strong and steady even in his old age tying thousands upon thousands of knots. I think about how each knot mattered, how each one contributed to the larger pattern even if he couldn't see the whole picture while he was working on it.

That's what I'm doing now with my life. I'm tying knots. Going to classes knot. Making friends knot. Learning new things knot. Experiencing failures and successes knot, knot, knot. I can't see the full pattern yet. I'm too close to the work, too focused on the individual threads. But I trust that eventually maybe years from now I'll be able to step back and see something beautiful.

I trust the process just like my grandfather taught me.

Last week my grandmother sent me a package. Inside was a small amount of wool thread blue, gold, red and green and a note in her shaky handwriting: Your grandfather was teaching you to weave before you left. He always hoped you would make your own carpet someday. When you're ready.

I'm not ready yet. I don't have the skill or the patience or the time. But I keep the thread in my desk drawer and sometimes I take it out and hold it feeling its texture, imagining the possibilities.

Someday I'll make my own carpet. It will have its own patterns and its own intentional imperfections. It will tell my story, knot by knot, color by color. It will be different from my grandfathers carpets but it will be connected to them too, part of the same tradition, the same family legacy.

For now I'm content to keep tying the knots of my daily life, trusting that their creating a pattern I can't yet see. Every choice, every experience, every person I meet their all threads in the carpet of my life. Some are bright and beautiful. Some are dark and difficult. But they all matter. They all belong.

Just like my grandfather said: one knot at a time and eventually something beautiful emerges.
