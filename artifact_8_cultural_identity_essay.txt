Cultural Identity Essay
Neshat Shahabi
First Year Writing Course

Weaving Two Worlds Together

Growing up as an Iranian American, I've always felt like I'm living in two different worlds at the same time. Its not a bad thing but it's definitely shaped who I am in ways I'm still figuring out. Sometimes these two cultures blend together perfectly and other times they clash in ways that make me question where I really belong.

At home I'm fully Iranian. My family speaks Farsi, we eat Persian food almost every night and my parents have certain expectations about respect, education and family that are deeply rooted in Iranian culture. When I walk through our front door I can smell the tahdig my mom makes, hear my dad watching Iranian news on his laptop and see the Persian carpets that have been in our family for generations. This is the world where I understand all the cultural references, where I know exactly how to behave and where I feel completely comfortable.

But the moment I step outside I switch into my American identity. I speak English without an accent, I understand pop culture references and I navigate school and social situations the way any other American student would. My friends don't always understand why I cant go out on school nights or why my parents are so strict about certain things. They don't get why family gatherings are such a big deal or why I have to call my grandparents in Iran every single week.

The interesting part is how these two identities have started to merge as I've gotten older. I used to try to keep them completely separate because I was worried that my American friends wouldn't understand my Iranian side and my Iranian relatives wouldn't approve of my American habits. But now I'm realizing that both parts make me who I am and I don't have to choose one over the other.

For example I've learned to appreciate the Persian concept of taarof which is this complicated system of politeness and hospitality that doesn't really have an English equivalent. At the same time I value the American emphasis on independence and speaking your mind. Sometimes these values conflict like when taarof tells me to refuse an offer three times to be polite but American directness says just to say what you actually want. I've had to learn when to use which approach and honestly that flexibility has become one of my strengths.

Food is probably where my dual identity shows up the most clearly. I'll pack Persian food for lunch like ghormeh sabzi or tahchin and my friends are always curious about what I'm eating. At first I was embarrassed because it looked and smelled different from their sandwiches and pizza. But now I'm proud to share my culture with them. I've even had friends over for Persian dinners and they love it. At the same time I genuinely enjoy American food too. I'm not pretending to like burgers and pizza just to fit in I actually do.

Language is another big part of my identity. I'm fluent in Farsi which connects me to my heritage and lets me communicate with my extended family in Iran. But I think in English now and sometimes I struggle to express complex ideas in Farsi. My parents worry that I'm losing my connection to the language and honestly their not completely wrong. It takes effort to maintain both languages but its worth it because each language carries different parts of who I am.

The hardest part about having a dual identity is feeling like I'm never quite enough for either side. Some of my Iranian relatives think I'm too Americanized that I don't understand or appreciate Iranian culture the way I should. Meanwhile some Americans see me as foreign or other no matter how fluent my English is or how well I understand American culture. There have been moments where I felt like I was stuck in the middle not fully accepted by either culture.

But I've come to realize that being in the middle isn't a bad place to be. It actually gives me a unique perspective that most people don't have. I can see both cultures from the inside and the outside at the same time. I can appreciate what's beautiful about each one while also recognizing their flaws. I can serve as a bridge between people who might not otherwise understand each other.

My Iranian American identity has taught me empathy. When I see immigrants struggling to adapt to American culture I understand their experience because I've watched my parents go through it. When I see Americans confused by cultural practices that seem strange to them I can explain and help them understand. I've learned to be comfortable with complexity and ambiguity which is a valuable skill in an increasingly interconnected world.

Looking forward I don't think I'll ever have to choose between being Iranian and being American. I'm both and that's okay. I'm learning to embrace the contradictions and the complexity. I'm proud of my heritage and the values my parents have passed down to me and I'm also grateful for the opportunities and freedoms I have as an American. Both cultures have shaped me and both will continue to influence who I become.

Being Iranian American means I get to celebrate Nowruz and Thanksgiving, speak two languages, appreciate two different styles of music and art and understand two different worldviews. It means I have family on two continents and feel at home in two very different places. Most importantly it means I've learned that identity isn't about picking sides its about weaving together all the different threads that make you who you are just like the intricate patterns in a Persian carpet.
