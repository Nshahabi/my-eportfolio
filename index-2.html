A Good Life Through the Lens

NESHAT SHAHABI

Hey there,

Welcome to my ePortfolio. I decided to use photography as my theme because I love how taking pictures and living a good life actually have so much in common. When you take a photo, you have to think about what to focus on, how much light to let in, how to arrange everything in the frame, and then how to develop it into something beautiful. Living well is pretty similar when you think about it. You have to figure out what matters to you, stay open to new experiences, balance all the different parts of your life, and keep growing as a person.

This ePortfolio is basically a collection of my journey through this course on the good life. But it goes beyond just what we did in class. This semester really made me think hard about big questions like what makes life worth living, how we find happiness and meaning, and why relationships and values matter so much. These are questions people have been asking forever, and I finally got the chance to explore them for myself.

The Focus page is all about figuring out what really matters and where to put my energy. Just like a photographer chooses what to focus on in a shot, I had to learn what deserves my attention in life. On that page you will find stuff about my character strengths, my cultural identity, and how I have gotten better at making intentional choices instead of just going through the motions. The VIA Character Strengths test really helped me understand myself better.

On the Exposure page, I share some really personal stuff. This page is about being vulnerable and open to experiences, kind of like how a camera needs to let in the right amount of light. I included my interview with my mom about what a good life means to her, a gratitude letter I wrote to my aunt Bita, and some discussion posts about happiness and love. Getting honest about my feelings and connections with people has taught me so much about what wellbeing really means.

The Composition page shows how I try to balance everything in my life. Work, relationships, personal growth, helping others. It is like arranging all the elements in a photograph so they work together instead of clashing. I put my research paper about dogs and happiness on this page, along with a video project from another class and my volunteer work. These pieces show how everything in life connects, even when it does not seem like it at first.

Development is the last themed page, and it is about growth and change. In photography, development is when you turn a blank negative into an actual picture. For me, this page is about how I have changed my thinking about happiness and the good life throughout this semester. I included reflections on resilience, my family immigration story, and how my ideas have evolved. Growth takes time and I am still figuring things out.

Making this ePortfolio has honestly been one of the most meaningful projects I have done in college. It is not just an assignment. By pulling together work from different classes, my personal life, and this course, I started seeing patterns I never noticed before. A good life is not something you achieve once and then you are done. It is an ongoing practice of paying attention to what matters, staying open, keeping things balanced, and always growing.

I made this ePortfolio for a bunch of different people. Obviously my professor needs to see it for a grade. But I also made it for my future self so I can look back and remember what I learned. I made it for my family and friends who have supported me. And I made it for anyone else who is trying to figure out what makes life good and meaningful. If you are reading this, I hope something here speaks to you and helps you think about your own life in a new way.

Thanks for checking out my work. I hope it makes you see your own life through a fresh lens.

With gratitude,
Neshat Shahabi

Focus

This page is all about how I learned to recognize my strengths, get clear on my values, and make better choices about where I spend my time. Through different tests, reflections, and personal stories, I got a much clearer picture of who I am and what I want my life to be about.

Assessment Results

Person-Activity Fit Diagnostic

This is a diagnostic tool we used in class from our positive psychology readings. It helps you figure out which happiness activities actually match your personality and life situation. My results showed that gratitude, savoring moments, and doing kind things for people worked best for me. But certain social activities were not really a good fit because I am more introverted. I put this here because it is a practical example of using self knowledge to make better choices. It is not enough to just know who you are. You have to actually use that knowledge to decide how to spend your time. The screenshot of my results reminds me that good focusing means matching what you do with who you actually are, not who you think you should be.

Exposure

In photography, exposure is all about how much light you let into the camera. Too little and the picture is dark and boring. Too much and everything is washed out. Living well needs the same kind of balance. You have to open yourself up to experiences, emotions, and connections with people, but you also need healthy boundaries. This page has artifacts that show my journey toward being more vulnerable, grateful, and emotionally honest. It is about learning to show my real self to the world and to let the world actually affect me instead of keeping everything at a distance.

Through our conversation, I learned about sacrifices she made that I never really understood before. She left her home country, had to rebuild everything in a new place, and put what my siblings and I needed ahead of her own career goals. She shared stuff she usually keeps private, and I let myself really feel how much she has been through. I chose this because it shows how opening yourself to other people's stories makes you understand so much more about what a meaningful life can look like. It is not just about your own experience.

Composition

Composition in photography is about arranging all the elements in your shot. Creating balance, guiding where the viewer looks, making different pieces work together. A well composed life needs the same thing. You have to integrate the different parts like school, relationships, work, fun, helping others, and rest. This page has artifacts that show how I learned to see connections between things that seemed totally separate before. And how to arrange these pieces so my life feels like it makes sense instead of being all over the place and chaotic.

Final Reflection

Creating this ePortfolio has been one of the most meaningful things I have done in college. Not because it was the hardest or took the most time. But because it asked me to do something different than usual. Most assignments want me to show I know the material. This one wanted me to show I understand myself. Building this portfolio taught me as much as any of the stuff inside it.

What I Learned About Myself

The biggest surprise was seeing patterns I never noticed before. When I was going through potential artifacts to include, I realized the ones I felt most drawn to all had something in common. They all involved bringing ideas together, making connections between different things, and translating between different ways of knowing stuff. The interview with my mom connected personal and scholarly views. The dog research paper linked studies to real life decisions. The video project bridged different generations. The resilience paper tied theory to actual experience.

This pattern showed me something important about myself. I am most engaged and most myself when I am connecting things and finding how separate stuff actually fits together. This matters in practical ways. It helps me get why certain classes energize me while others drain me, even when they are equally hard. It points toward careers that might really work for me. Most importantly, it gives me words to explain what I need to do well in life.

I also learned my cultural background has shaped me way more than I realized. My Iranian side values education, family loyalty, hospitality, and pushing through hard times. My American side values individual achievement, making your own choices, being authentic, and pursuing happiness. I have been trying to balance these my whole life without really admitting the tension. Working on the Focus page, especially thinking about my cultural identity essay and the Nowruz photo, helped me see I do not have to pick one. My good life will involve honoring both. Finding ways to be ambitious and connected. Independent and loyal. Authentically me and deeply tied to tradition.

What Worked in Building This ePortfolio

The photography metaphor worked way better than I expected. At first I worried it would feel forced or fake. But as I built each page, the metaphor actually helped me think deeper. Focus, exposure, composition, and development are not just clever categories. They really do represent different parts of living well. Thinking through the photography parallel helped me notice things I might have missed.

Having to include artifacts from different places was crucial. This course, other classes, personal stuff. If I only used things from this class, it would have felt like a fancy final exam. By bringing in my writing course essay, my psychology reflection, my communications video, and my volunteer work, I could see how thinking about the good life shows up everywhere once you start looking. It is not just one class or one subject.

Writing real reflections for each artifact made me go deeper than surface level. I could not just say this shows I value family or this proves I like psychology. I had to explain why each piece mattered, what it revealed, and how it connects to my view of the good life. This deep thinking turned the artifacts from just evidence into actual insight.

What Did Not Work and What I Would Do Differently

I struggled with perfectionism the whole time. I kept changing which artifacts to use, rewriting reflections over and over, second guessing everything. At some point I had to accept this portfolio would never feel done the way a regular paper does. It is a snapshot of my thinking right now. And that thinking will keep changing. If I started over, I would try to accept the work in progress nature earlier. Seeing each version as a draft instead of waiting for some perfect final thing.

I also wish I started picking artifacts earlier. I waited pretty late in the semester to really choose things. That meant I was deciding kind of frantically. If I had been collecting possibilities throughout the semester, keeping notes on why certain pieces felt right, the final choices would have felt more natural and less rushed. The lesson is good work comes from paying attention over time, not cramming at the last minute.

Another hard thing was writing for multiple audiences at once. I wanted this to matter to my future self, impress my teacher, make sense to my family, and help other students. Trying to satisfy everyone sometimes pulled my writing in different directions. Looking back, I think I should have focused on writing to my future self and trusted that being real would work for the other readers too.

What This Project Means for My Understanding of the Good Life

This ePortfolio has made several things really clear about wellbeing and what it means to flourish. First, the good life is not a place you get to. It is an ongoing practice. Something you keep adjusting like focus, exposure, and arrangement as things change. There is no point where you arrive and can just coast. Living well takes constant attention and adjustment.

Second, knowing yourself is important but not enough. Understanding my strengths, values, and tendencies through tests like VIA and MBTI was helpful. But that knowledge only matters if I actually use it to make better choices. The Person Activity Fit Diagnostic showed me which activities fit my personality. But I still have to actually do those activities. Information does not automatically create change.

Third, the good life is basically about relationships. The artifacts I am most proud of are all about connection with other people. The interview with my mom, the gratitude letter to Bita, the family immigration story, the volunteer work. Even the individual achievements like my papers happened because of teachers, family support, and communities of people sharing knowledge. This tells me that thinking about wellbeing as just individual is incomplete. We do well in relationship with others or we do not do well at all.

Fourth, hard times and discomfort are not things blocking the good life. They are often essential to it. The artifacts that taught me the most all involved being vulnerable and taking risks. The first year struggles essay, the resilience reflection, the scary experience of reading my gratitude letter out loud. Growth needs you to go beyond what is comfortable. Which means some difficulty is just part of development. Not something to avoid.

Finally, meaning matters more than happiness, but they are connected. My early definition focused heavily on happiness. Feeling good, enjoying life, having fun. Through this course and making this ePortfolio, I have come to see that meaning is actually the deeper goal. The sense that your life matters and contributes to something beyond just you. Interestingly, research shows that going after meaning is more likely to create lasting happiness than chasing happiness directly. This is the happiness paradox my discussion posts explored. Building this portfolio made that paradox real to me personally, not just something interesting to think about.

Looking Forward

This ePortfolio will be something I come back to over time. When I face big decisions about career, relationships, where to live, how to spend my time, I can look at these reflections and artifacts to remind myself what I care about and who I want to be. When I lose focus, I can return to the Focus page. When I am getting too closed off or careful, the Exposure page can remind me why vulnerability and openness matter. When my life feels all over the place, Composition can help me look for integration. And when I am impatient with my progress, Development can remind me growth takes time and setbacks are part of it.

I am also excited to keep building on this. This is not a final statement. It is the beginning of a longer conversation with myself about what makes life worth living. I plan to come back and update this portfolio sometimes. Adding new artifacts, changing reflections as my understanding grows, tracking how my definition of the good life shifts with age and experience. In five years, ten years, twenty years, it will be really interesting to see how my thinking has changed, what stayed the same, and what is different.

Most importantly, this project showed me that living well is active and intentional. It does not just happen by default or luck. It needs the kind of careful attention a photographer brings to their work. Deciding what to focus on, how much light to let in, how to arrange things, and how to develop the potential into something meaningful and beautiful. That is the work of a whole life. And I am grateful I got to start here.
