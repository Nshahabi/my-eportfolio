Haft Seen Reflection
Neshat Shahabi

New Beginnings and Old Traditions

This photo is from our family's haft seen table last Nowruz. For anyone who doesn't know Nowruz is the Persian New Year and its one of the most important celebrations in Iranian culture. It marks the first day of spring and the beginning of a new year on the Persian calendar. The haft seen is a special table setting that includes seven items that all start with the letter S in Farsi and each one symbolizes something different like health, wealth, love, beauty or rebirth.

Every year my mom spends days preparing our haft seen. She's very particular about it because its a tradition that's been passed down in our family for generations. In this photo you can see the sabzeh which is wheat or lentil sprouts growing in a dish. Theres also senjed which are dried fruit from a lotus tree, seer which is garlic, seeb which is apples, somaq which is sumac spice, serkeh which is vinegar and samanu which is a sweet paste made from wheat. We also add a mirror, candles, painted eggs, goldfish, coins and a book of poetry usually Hafez.

When I was younger I used to think the haft seen was just a pretty decoration. I liked looking at all the colorful items and the way my mom arranged everything so carefully. But I didn't really understand why we did it or what it meant. Now that I'm older and especially after being in America for several years I've come to appreciate it in a completely different way.

The haft seen represents renewal and hope. Each spring we set up this table as a reminder that we have a chance to start fresh, to grow like the sabzeh, to be patient like the process of making samanu and to stay grounded in our values. Its a physical representation of the things we hope for in the coming year.

What strikes me most now is how this tradition connects me to my heritage in a way that feels really important. Being Iranian American means I'm constantly navigating between two cultures and sometimes I worry about losing touch with my Iranian roots. But when we set up the haft seen every year its like anchoring myself to something that goes back hundreds of years. My grandparents did this, their grandparents did this and now I'm doing it too.

I think about my grandfather a lot during Nowruz. He used to say that traditions are like threads that connect us to our past and to each other. Without them we'd be adrift not knowing where we came from or who we are. The haft seen is one of those threads for me. Even though I'm thousands of miles away from where my family originally came from, even though I speak English more than Farsi now, even though so much about my life is American this tradition reminds me that I'm still connected to Iran and to my family's history.

Setting up the haft seen has also become a way for me to share my culture with others. I've had American friends come over during Nowruz and they're always fascinated by the table and what everything means. Its given me opportunities to educate people about Persian culture and to feel proud of my heritage instead of trying to hide it or downplay it.

Theres something really beautiful about celebrating the new year at the exact moment of the spring equinox when day and night are equal. Its like the universe itself is in balance for that one moment and were acknowledging it. Most Americans celebrate New Year on January first which is kind of arbitrary if you think about it. But Nowruz is tied to nature, to the actual rebirth that happens every spring when plants start growing again and the world comes back to life.

This particular haft seen in the photo is special to me because it was the first one we set up after my grandfather passed away. I remember sitting in front of it during the exact moment of the new year feeling really sad that he wasn't there to celebrate with us. But I also felt his presence in the tradition itself. He had helped my grandmother set up their haft seen every year for decades and now we were continuing that tradition without him. It was painful but also comforting in a way.

My mom placed his photo next to the haft seen that year which you can see in the picture. She also included his favorite poetry book instead of the usual Hafez. Those small additions made the table feel more personal more connected to our specific family story rather than just a general cultural tradition.

I think the haft seen is a perfect example of how rituals and traditions contribute to a good life. They give us a sense of continuity and belonging. They connect us to something larger than ourselves. They create moments where families come together and remember what matters. They mark the passage of time in a meaningful way.

Every time I look at this photo I'm reminded that I'm part of a long chain of people who have celebrated Nowruz and set up the haft seen. One day I'll probably set up a haft seen for my own kids and explain to them what each item means just like my mom explained it to me. And hopefully they'll appreciate it and pass it on to their kids too.

The items on the haft seen change slightly from year to year. Sometimes we add different books or different decorative elements. But the core tradition stays the same. I think thats what makes it powerful. Its flexible enough to adapt to our current lives but rooted enough to feel authentic and meaningful.

Living in America has actually made me appreciate Nowruz more not less. When you're surrounded by your culture all the time you might take it for granted. But when you're living somewhere else and you have to make an effort to maintain your traditions you start to see their value more clearly. Setting up the haft seen every year is an active choice we make to stay connected to our Iranian identity.

This photo also reminds me of the concept of renewal which is central to Nowruz. The idea that we can start fresh that we can grow and change and become better versions of ourselves. Thats something I've needed to believe in especially during difficult times. When I was struggling to adjust to life in America or when I was dealing with academic stress or personal challenges I would think about the sabzeh growing on our haft seen table. If those tiny seeds can push through and grow then so can I.

I keep this photo on my phone and sometimes when I'm feeling disconnected or lost I look at it. It grounds me. It reminds me where I come from and what I value. It tells me that I'm part of something bigger than just my individual life. I'm part of a family, a culture, a history.

The haft seen isn't just a decoration. Its a statement. Its saying that despite everything despite moving to a new country, despite all the changes and challenges were still here, were still Persian and were still celebrating our traditions. Were still honoring our past while living in our present. Were still hoping for a better future.

And I think thats what living a good life is partly about. Its about knowing who you are and where you come from. Its about maintaining connections to the things and people that matter. Its about having rituals and traditions that give your life structure and meaning. Its about renewal and hope and the belief that things can always get better.

This haft seen photo represents all of that for me. Its a reminder of my heritage, my family, my values and my hope for the future. Its a symbol of continuity in a life that sometimes feels chaotic and disconnected. And its beautiful which matters too because beauty is part of a good life as well.
